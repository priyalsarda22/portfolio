Wearable EMG & Bioimpedance System

Detecting Early Signs of Typing-Related Musculoskeletal Disorders through Real-Time Posture and Fatigue Monitoring

Fall 2024 | Georgia Tech

Project Overview

As someone who spends countless hours coding, I've personally experienced wrist fatigue and understood the real risks of developing carpal tunnel syndrome or tendonitis. That's why I worked with a team of 4 to create a wearable device that detects the early warning signs of musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs) before they become serious.

We combined two complementary technologies: EMG to monitor wrist posture in real-time, and bioimpedance analysis to detect muscle fatigue. I led the EMG subsystem development while also contributing to the bioimpedance circuit. The device provides immediate LCD feedback, alerting users when poor typing posture is detected and tracking fatigue levels throughout the workday.

Our Innovation: A preventative wearable device that detects both incorrect posture and early-stage muscle fatigue, providing real-time intervention before MSDs develop.

The Problem

Clinical Need

  • 75% of office workers experience musculoskeletal disorders
  • Keyboard use exceeding 20 hours/week significantly increases carpal tunnel syndrome risk
  • Non-neutral wrist positioning is the largest contributing factor to MSDs
  • Most interventions happen after symptoms appear, when damage has already occurred

Our Approach

  • Real-time EMG monitoring of wrist posture during typing
  • Bioimpedance analysis to detect early muscle fatigue
  • Immediate visual feedback for posture correction
  • Preventative intervention before tissue damage occurs

System Design & How We Built It

The device operates in three stages: (1) records baseline bioimpedance values before typing, (2) continuously monitors wrist posture via EMG during typing and displays LCD alerts for poor positioning, (3) compares post-session bioimpedance against baseline to assess muscle fatigue.

Circuit Architecture

The system uses two complementary circuits powered by a battery-operated Arduino: the EMG and bioimpedance subsystems. Real-time feedback displays on an LCD screen showing typing posture and tissue health insights.

EMG Circuit Components

I designed the EMG circuit to monitor the flexor carpi ulnaris muscle - its overactivation indicates poor wrist posture during typing. The circuit amplifies small muscle signals (0.1-5 mV) to Arduino's input range (0-5V) while filtering out noise.

EMG circuit architecture with AD8220 instrumentation amplifier, high-pass and low-pass filters, and operational amplifiers for signal conditioning

Stage 1: Instrumentation Amplifier

  • Component: AD8220 or AD623N
  • Selected for excellent common-mode rejection ratio (CMRR)
  • Critical for removing power line noise
  • Differential input amplifies voltage difference between muscle electrodes

Stage 2-3: Filtering

  • High-Pass Filter (5 Hz cutoff): Removes DC offset and motion artifacts
  • Low-Pass Filter (500 Hz cutoff): Eliminates high-frequency electrical noise
  • Preserves EMG signal band (10-500 Hz)

Stage 4: Additional Amplification

  • Operational amplifiers provide additional gain stages
  • Matches Arduino's 0-5V analog input range
  • Enables accurate digitization of muscle signals

Bioimpedance Circuit Components

I also helped develop the bioimpedance subsystem to evaluate muscle fatigue by measuring tissue impedance. We used a four-wire technique to minimize skin impedance influence. The AD5940 chip generates an AC signal applied to the forearm while measuring current and voltage to calculate tissue impedance.

Bioimpedance circuit using AD5940 chip with four-electrode configuration for accurate tissue impedance measurement

Dual-Frequency Measurement

We designed the system to monitor bioimpedance at two frequencies:

  • 5 kHz: Primarily assesses extracellular pathways
  • 100 kHz: Provides insights into intracellular pathways
  • h-value ratio: h = ΔR₁₀₀ₖₕz / ΔR₅ₖₕz (baseline: 1.0 for no fatigue)
  • Changes in ratio reflect fluid accumulation in muscle tissue, a key indicator of fatigue

Electrode Placement

For the EMG circuit, I placed positive and negative electrodes on the flexor carpi ulnaris muscle belly with the reference electrode on the elbow for stable grounding. For bioimpedance, we positioned current-injecting electrodes at forearm ends and voltage-sensing electrodes centrally to minimize skin impedance impact.

My Contributions

I led EMG subsystem development while contributing to the bioimpedance system. My work involved three phases: building the EMG circuit, analyzing data in MATLAB to determine post-processing requirements, and implementing real-time algorithms on Arduino.

Phase 1: EMG Circuit Design and Construction

I built the EMG circuit with a four-stage architecture that amplifies muscle signals (0.1-5 mV) to Arduino's range (0-5V) while filtering noise. I tested multiple electrode placements to find the optimal position on the flexor carpi ulnaris for clearest signal differentiation between good and bad posture.

Phase 2: Data Collection and MATLAB Signal Analysis

I collected raw EMG data and imported it into MATLAB to understand signal behavior and determine required post-processing steps. I prototyped all algorithms in MATLAB before Arduino implementation.

MATLAB signal processing pipeline showing raw EMG data, rectification, smoothing, RMS calculation, and peak detection for posture classification

What I Did in MATLAB:

  • Raw Signal Analysis: Loaded raw EMG voltage data and visualized it to understand signal characteristics, noise patterns, and baseline drift
  • Signal Preprocessing Pipeline: Implemented mean subtraction (DC offset removal), absolute value transformation (full-wave rectification), and signal smoothing using moving average filters
  • Feature Extraction: Calculated Root Mean Square (RMS) amplitude as a measure of overall muscle activation level
  • Peak Detection Algorithm: Developed peak counting methods to characterize activation patterns - increased wave frequency correlates with sustained improper wrist positioning
  • Dual-Threshold Classification: Created two complementary thresholds: (1) RMS amplitude threshold for sudden spikes, (2) Peak frequency threshold >50 peaks for sustained poor posture
Key Insight: I discovered that a single threshold approach was insufficient. Sudden spikes could indicate quick movements, while sustained activation at moderate levels better indicated typing with extended/flexed wrist. The dual-threshold combining amplitude and frequency proved essential.

Phase 3: Arduino Implementation for Real-Time Wearable Operation

I translated MATLAB algorithms into optimized C++ code for Arduino, enabling real-time signal processing on battery power. This made the device truly wearable - continuously collecting EMG data, processing it, classifying posture, and displaying results without computer connection.

Technical Challenges & Solutions

  • Memory Constraints: Signal buffer consumed >85% of available SRAM. I optimized data structures, used appropriate variable types, and implemented circular buffers to minimize memory footprint
  • Real-Time Processing: Implemented efficient moving average filters and running RMS calculations that updated with each new sample rather than recomputing entire buffers
  • ADC Sampling: Configured analog-to-digital converter to sample >1000 Hz for EMG signals while balancing processing constraints
  • LCD Display Integration: Programmed LCD to continuously display 'Good' or 'Bad' based on real-time classification using I2C protocol to minimize pin usage
  • Threshold Calibration: Implemented adaptive threshold system accounting for individual differences in muscle activation
The Result: A working real-time EMG monitoring system running on battery power that accurately detects poor typing posture and provides immediate visual feedback without needing a computer connection. The system successfully differentiates between good posture (neutral wrist) and bad postures including reaching for keys, typing at angles, wrist rotation, and wrist resting. The Arduino-based implementation made the device truly wearable and practical for everyday use.

Bioimpedance System: Fatigue Detection

While I focused primarily on the EMG subsystem, I also collaborated with my team on developing the bioimpedance subsystem for muscle fatigue detection. We wanted this system to provide a complementary measurement that detects microscopic changes in tissue composition before they manifest as macroscopic symptoms.

Technology and Measurement Principle

We learned that bioimpedance provides a non-invasive means of monitoring tissue composition changes in electrical resistance due to extracellular and intracellular fluids. AC current travels differently through these two fluid spaces: low-frequency currents pass through extracellular fluid, while higher-frequency currents can penetrate through the cell. At a cellular level, the cell membrane acts as a capacitor which creates a barrier that prevents low frequency current from passing through. As frequency increases, the capacitive reactance of the membrane decreases, and signals can thereby pass through.

When muscles become fatigued, metabolic byproducts accumulate in extracellular fluid, decreasing electrical resistance. That's why we chose bioimpedance - it detects these microscopic changes in the extracellular region due to fatigue before they manifest as macroscopic symptoms, offering a non-invasive method to track muscle stress which potentially causes overuse injuries over time.

Clinical Significance

  • Function: Muscle fatigue detection through tissue impedance changes
  • Output: Real-time impedance values at 5 kHz displayed on LCD
  • Impact: Detects muscle inflammation and edema before they manifest as pain
  • Four-electrode setup: Accounts for skin impedance, providing more accurate tissue measurements

Physical Housing Design

We designed a simplistic 3D printed enclosure to house all the electrical components. We decided the device should be worn on the upper arm, so we created an interface between the enclosure and the arm that features a curved surface to increase the comfort and fit of the packaging.

The enclosure houses:

  • Arduino microcontroller and battery power supply
  • EMG conditioning circuitry (AD8220 instrumentation amplifier, filters)
  • Bioimpedance circuitry (AD5940 chip, ADICUP3029 baseboard)
  • LCD display for real-time feedback
  • Wired electrodes connecting to forearm muscles

We added larger holes on either side of the enclosure to facilitate more efficient wiring configurations and allow the electrodes to be connected into the components within the box and also to the flexor carpi ulnaris. I made more cutouts to decrease the 3D printing time and cost of production without compromising structural integrity and aesthetics. We held all of these components together with a sleeve.

Testing & Validation Results

EMG Posture Detection Testing

I conducted extensive testing of my EMG system using various controlled posture conditions. I wanted to ensure the system could reliably detect different types of poor posture, so I tested it across multiple users and typing scenarios.

Real-time demonstration of EMG system detecting good vs. bad posture and displaying feedback on LCD

Good Posture Conditions (Correctly Classified)

  • Neutral wrist position (0° extension/flexion)
  • Hands floating above keyboard with minimal wrist activation
  • Proper ergonomic typing form

Bad Posture Conditions (Correctly Detected)

  • Reaching over for keys (excessive extension)
  • Typing at an angle (radial/ulnar deviation)
  • Sustained wrist rotation
  • Resting wrists on keyboard pad while typing

The dual-threshold approach I developed proved highly effective - my system achieved reliable detection across different users and typing styles. I programmed the microcontroller to process the EMG signal data in real time and display 'Good' or 'Bad' on the LCD screen to alert users of their posture.

Bioimpedance Fatigue Detection Testing

We tested our device by placing the electrodes on our forearm tissue and continuously gathering data at each chosen frequency, 5 kHz and 100 kHz, over the span of several minutes. We wanted to validate that the bioimpedance measurements could actually detect fatigue.

Experimental Protocol

  • Minute 1: Baseline measurement (forearm at rest, non-fatigued)
  • Minutes 2-3: Intentional fatigue through repetitive typing motions
  • Minute 4: Post-fatigue measurement (at rest to minimize motion artifacts)
