Wrist Flexor Muscle Activation EMG Study

Investigating the ergonomic impact of digital vs. traditional writing tools through surface electromyography analysis of wrist flexor muscle fatigue

Fall 2024 | Georgia Tech

EMG Circuit Design Arduino MATLAB Signal Processing Statistical Analysis Biomechanics
← Back to Projects

Project Overview

With digital styluses like the Apple Pencil becoming increasingly prevalent in classrooms and workplaces, I wanted to understand their ergonomic impact compared to traditional writing tools. With a team of 4, I conducted a comprehensive study investigating whether writing medium affects wrist flexor muscle fatigue using surface electromyography (EMG).

Research Question: Does writing with an Apple Pencil on iPad produce significantly different wrist flexor muscle activation compared to a mechanical pencil on paper?

Hypothesis: We hypothesized that the Apple Pencil would result in lower muscle activation due to the smooth glass surface requiring less grip force than paper friction.

Experimental Design & Methodology

Study Design

We implemented a within-subjects experimental design where each of the 52 participants served as their own control. This approach minimizes inter-individual variability and increases statistical power. I helped recruit right-handed Georgia Tech students aged 18-23 with no recent wrist or hand injuries.

Sample Size Determination

  • Used G*Power software (version 3.1.9.6)
  • Target statistical power: 95%
  • Significance level: α = 0.05
  • Required sample: 52 participants

Testing Protocol

  • MVC Normalization: 3 trials, 5 sec each
  • Randomization via coin flip
  • 100-word standardized passage
  • 1-minute rest between trials

Electrode Placement Protocol

Electrode Configuration

  • Positive electrode: Placed on the wrist over the flexor carpi ulnaris
  • Negative electrode: Positioned on the muscle belly of the wrist flexor
  • Ground electrode: Attached to the left hip bone for common reference

EMG Data Acquisition System

Circuit Design & Hardware

We designed and implemented a custom EMG acquisition circuit using the BMED 3110 circuit board with carefully selected components for optimal signal conditioning.

Signal Conditioning Stages

  1. Instrumentation Amplifier (AD623N): First-stage amplification with adjustable gain G₁ = 1 + 100kΩ/R₃ to boost the weak EMG signal (typically 0.1-5 mV) while rejecting common-mode noise
  2. Bandpass Filtering: Low-pass filter (1 nF capacitor) removed high-frequency noise above 500 Hz, while high-pass filter (10 µF capacitor) eliminated motion artifacts and DC offset below 15 Hz, resulting in a 15-500 Hz bandwidth optimal for EMG signals
  3. Secondary Amplification (Dual Op-Amp TLC2272A): Additional gain stage G₂ = R₅/R₄ provided final amplification for optimal Arduino input range (overall gain = 3.3 kΩ)

Data Acquisition with Arduino & MATLAB

I used an Arduino Uno R3 microcontroller to digitize the conditioned analog EMG signals. For real-time data collection and visualization, I utilized the MATLAB Multiport Data Collection app (version R2024a), which allowed me to:

  • Monitor live EMG signals in real-time to verify signal quality
  • Record continuous EMG data streams throughout each writing task
  • Export raw data for post-processing and analysis
  • Troubleshoot electrode placement issues by observing signal characteristics

My Role: Data Processing & Statistical Analysis

After collecting EMG data from all 52 participants, I took primary responsibility for processing and analyzing the complete dataset. This involved several systematic steps to transform raw voltage signals into meaningful physiological measurements.

  1. Data Organization: Exported all raw EMG data from MATLAB into Microsoft Excel, organizing into a structured spreadsheet with separate columns for each participant, trial type (MVC, Apple Pencil, or Mechanical Pencil), and time-stamped voltage readings
  2. Signal Processing - RMS Calculation: For each writing trial, I calculated the root mean square (RMS) of the EMG signal, which provides a measure of signal magnitude that correlates with muscle activation level
  3. MVC Normalization: To enable valid comparison across participants, I normalized each Flexor RMS value by dividing it by that participant's average maximum voluntary contraction: % Activation = (Flexor RMS / Average MVC) × 100%
  4. Statistical Testing: Conducted paired two-sample t-tests (α = 0.05) to compare muscle activation between conditions, using both one-tailed and two-tailed tests for comprehensive analysis

Interpretation: The mechanical pencil consistently produced marginally higher muscle activation across participants, supporting our hypothesis that the Apple Pencil offers ergonomic benefits. Key Takeaway

Our research revealed an ergonomic-performance trade-off: mechanical pencils enable faster writing but require slightly more muscular effort, while Apple Pencils reduce muscle strain but slow writing speed.

Practical Applications

  • Educational Technology: Schools implementing iPad programs should consider the speed-comfort trade-off. For timed assessments, traditional tools may be preferable; for extended note-taking, digital tools may reduce fatigue.
  • Occupational Health: Individuals with repetitive strain injuries or carpal tunnel syndrome may benefit from digital stylus use during extended writing sessions.
  • Product Design: Digital stylus manufacturers could focus on improving tactile feedback to enhance writing speed while maintaining ergonomic advantages.
